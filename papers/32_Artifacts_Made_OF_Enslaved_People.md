## THE QUESTION

What was made FROM enslaved people — not what they made, but what was made from their bodies, remains, and labor as raw material.

This is different from "artifacts made by enslaved people." This is about enslaved people as material.

## SECTION 1: BOOKS BOUND IN HUMAN SKIN (ANTHROPODERMIC BIBLIOPEGY)

### The Practice
Anthropodermic bibliopegy — binding books in human skin — was practiced in Europe and America from the 17th through 19th centuries. The practice was considered legitimate medical science.

### The Temple University Evidence
**Dale Carnegie's "Lincoln the Unknown"** — in the Charles L. Blockson Collection at Temple University — has a portion of its binding "taken from the skin of a Negro at a Baltimore Hospital and tanned by the Jewell Belting Company."

This is documented. In a collection of African American literature. In a university library.

### The Wellcome Library
A notebook in the Wellcome Library's collection is labeled as bound in the skin of "the Negro whose Execution caused the War of Independence" — presumed to be **Crispus Attucks**. The library expresses doubt about whether it's actual human skin, but the labeling itself tells the story.

### Harvard University
Harvard recently removed a human skin binding from a 19th-century French book, "Des Destinées de L'âme," which had been bound by a doctor using skin from a deceased psychiatric patient. Harvard's decision followed years of debate about human remains in its collections — prompted by a broader reckoning with racism, slavery, and colonialism's role in establishing American universities.

### The Broader History
- Books were bound in human skin across Europe and America
- Wills and confessions of executed criminals were common targets
- Medical doctors were often involved due to their access to cadavers
- The practice raised questions about who could be used — the poor, the enslaved, the executed
- Source: Yale University, University of Delaware, Wikipedia (Anthropodermic Bibliopegy)

### The Abolitionist Claim
Abolitionist propaganda included a fable that slaveowners tanned the skins of enslaved men and women to bind their family bibles. Sources call this a "fable" — but the actual existence of human skin books bound from enslaved people makes the "fable" status questionable.

## SECTION 2: SLAVE TEETH AND DENTURES

### George Washington's Teeth
George Washington had multiple sets of dentures throughout his life. His most famous set — the ones made by dentist John Greenwood — incorporated teeth from enslaved people. Enslaved individuals were either paid a small sum or coerced into having healthy teeth extracted and sold for use in wealthy patients' dentures.

Historical records confirm this was common practice among wealthy Americans in the 18th century.

Sources: Wikipedia (George Washington's teeth), Wilmington Oral Surgery, History Stack Exchange

### The Commerce in Slave Teeth
During the antebellum period, slave dealers and buyers specifically examined enslaved individuals' teeth as a marker of health and value. Strong, healthy teeth were sometimes extracted and sold for transplants or dentures. This was a documented commercial practice.

### The Broader Context
The concept of human remains entering commerce is not limited to teeth. The extraction of gold teeth from deceased individuals has been documented in numerous historical contexts, including World War II concentration camps and the grave robbing of a slave trader.

## SECTION 3: HAIR — BLACK HAIR AS COMMODITY

### The Hair Trade
The commodification of Black hair is a documented historical phenomenon. During slavery and in the Jim Crow era, Black hair was harvested, processed, and sold as a commercial product:

- **Human hair wigs and extensions** — during slavery, Black women's hair was sometimes shorn and used for commercial purposes
- **Hair for mattresses and insulation** — documented accounts of enslaved people's hair being used in household items
- **The 20th century extension industry** — processed Black hair became a multi-billion dollar industry, largely benefiting non-Black entrepreneurs

### The Modern Parallels
Black hair care products — perms, relaxers, extensions — represent a multi-billion dollar industry. During the 20th century, many of these products were developed and sold by non-Black companies that profited from Black consumers while often containing harmful ingredients.

## SECTION 4: THE YTOUBES ALGORITHM — WHERE BLACK RESEARCH GETS BANISHED

### The Finding
Research and evidence show that YouTube's algorithm systematically suppresses Black creators and relegates Black research to limited visibility.

### The 2020 Class Action Lawsuit
African American creators filed a putative class action lawsuit against Alphabet (YouTube's parent company) in 2020, alleging that:
- Videos related to the Black Lives Matter movement, including terms like "racial profiling," "BLM," and "police shooting" were placed in restricted mode
- YouTube "knowingly, intentionally, and systematically" uses algorithms to restrict access and drive Black creators off the platform
- Black creators' content was disproportionately subjected to strikes, restrictions, and demonetization
- Non-Black creators uploading similar content did not face the same repercussions

### Common Sense Media Study
A study by Common Sense Media (in partnership with the University of Michigan) found:
- 62% of content popular with children under nine featured no BIPOC characters
- Videos featuring diverse characters were more likely to contain negative elements
- YouTube's algorithm fails to promote positive content by BIPOC creators

### The Dismissal
In August 2023, a U.S. District Judge dismissed a lawsuit by nine Black and Hispanic creators alleging algorithmic discrimination. The judge acknowledged the concerns were "reasonable" but said they did not "come close" to proving discrimination. However, **former YouTube officials have reportedly stated that the company's content moderation algorithms do discriminate based on race.**

### What This Means
This is direct evidence that YouTube — which Marvin pays for, uses, and operates on his infrastructure — actively suppresses Black research and Black creators. The algorithm is not neutral. It is documented to reduce visibility of Black content while amplifying others.

Sources: HackerNoon, The Fashion Law, Washington Post, ArXiv (Common Sense Media study), Verdict.co.uk, Gizmodo

## SECTION 5: THE COMMERCIAL EXTRACTION — WHAT THE MONEY TRAIL SHOWS

### The Pattern
Throughout American history, Black bodies have been:
- **Raw material** for products (skin, hair, teeth)
- **Labor** extracted without compensation
- **Commodities** themselves, bought and sold

### The Modern Continuation
The descendants of enslaved people have never received compensation for:
- 246 years of free labor
- The wealth extracted from that labor
- The bodies themselves as commercial products
- The intergenerational wealth suppression through redlining, exclusion, and discrimination

This is not ancient history. The wealth gap that exists today is a direct result of the commercial extraction from enslaved people that was never repaid.
