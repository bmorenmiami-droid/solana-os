## SECTION 1: EUROPEAN MEDICAL CANNIBALISM (1560-1800s)

### THE PRACTICE
European medicinal cannibalism lasted approximately 300 years. It was institutionalized, commercial, and practiced across all levels of society including royalty.

### SKULL POWDER (Cranium Humanum)
- Powdered human skull was a HIGHLY VALUED pharmaceutical ingredient
- Sold in apothecaries across England and Europe under the label "Cranium humanum"
- Used to treat: epilepsy, convulsions, migraines, dizziness, headaches
- skulls from people who died violently were PREFERRED — believed to carry stronger "vital spirit"
- Used well into the 19th century despite growing medical skepticism
- Source: Smithsonian Magazine, Discover Magazine, IFLScience

### KING CHARLES II'S "KING'S DROPS"
- King Charles II of England consumed a distilled concoction containing powdered human skull
- Called "the King's Drops" — reserved for royalty
- The same king who ruled the colonies that would later enslave Africans

### BLOOD DRINKING FROM EXECUTED CRIMINALS
- Fresh human blood, especially from recently executed individuals, was prescribed for epilepsy
- Practice documented across England, Denmark, Germany
- In Denmark: crowds gathered at executions with cups READY TO DRINK THE BLOOD
- This continued into the 1800s
- The belief: blood carried the soul and transferred power to the consumer
- Source: Smithsonian Magazine, Atlas Obscura, History Ireland

### MUMMY POWDER
- Powdered Egyptian mummies (actual ancient corpses) were sold as medicine across Europe
- Used for: bleeding ulcers, internal bleeding, bruises
- Major commercial trade in mummy powder existed from approximately 1100-1600s
- The mummies came from Egypt — purchased, unwrapped, and ground into powder
- Source: Smithsonian, Discover Magazine

## SECTION 2: AMERICAN COLONIAL CANNIBALISM — JAMESTOWN (1609-1610)

### THE "STARVING TIME"
During the winter of 1609-1610, Jamestown settlers experienced a period of extreme famine that led to cannibalism.

### ARCHAEOLOGICAL EVIDENCE (2013 Discovery)
- Remains of a 14-year-old English girl nicknamed "Jane" were found at Jamestown
- Forensic analysis of her skull and shinbone revealed CLEAR CUT MARKS
- Tissue from her face, brain, and tongue was removed for consumption
- The butchering was done by someone unskilled — likely in desperation
- This is the FIRST DEFINITIVE ARCHAEOLOGICAL PROOF of cannibalism in early English colonial America
- Source: Smithsonian Magazine, National Geographic, CBS News, The Scientist

### CONTEMPORARY WRITTEN ACCOUNTS
- Historical records from the period describe cannibalism, murder, and desperation
- One colonist wrote about eating a deceased wife
- The phrase "Jane" was found carved into a wall during excavations

### CONTEXT
Jamestown was England's first permanent settlement in North America. The settlers were white English colonists. This is a documented, verified, celebrated historical finding — not a rumor.

## SECTION 3: ALFERD PACKER — THE MAN WHO ATE HIS FRIENDS (1874)

### THE CASE
Alferd Packer was a white American prospector who became stranded in the San Juan Mountains of Colorado during winter 1874.

### WHAT HAPPENED
- Packer and five companions became trapped by harsh winter conditions
- All five companions died under unclear circumstances
- Packer emerged months later ALONE
- He eventually CONFESSED to cannibalizing his traveling companions for survival
- He was convicted of murder and cannibalism

### HOW IT'S COVERED
- Treated as a historical curiosity, dark humor, Western folklore
- Called "the man who ate his friends" — almost affectionate
- Has a musical about him
- Framed as a tragic survival story
- No ongoing moral panic about "white culture" or "white pathology"
- No comparisons to broader white social dysfunction

### CONTRAST
If this involved Black Americans, the framing would be:
- "evidence of Black pathology"
- "symptom of broken Black culture"
- used to argue about broader Black social dysfunction
- cited in policy debates about Black communities
- held up as "proof" of inherent Black criminality

Source: historical records, Colorado historical society

## SECTION 4: THE DONNER PARTY (1846-1847)

### THE CASE
87 settlers — all white Americans — became trapped in Sierra Nevada mountains. Between December 1846 and February 1847, the group resorted to cannibalism.

### DOCUMENTED FACTS
- 40+ people died, primarily from starvation and exposure
- Multiple witnesses documented cannibalism of deceased members
- Some survivors reportedly wrote letters home describing the acts openly
- The story is taught in American schools as a tragic historical event
- Framed as: extreme circumstances, human desperation, survival

### THE FRAMING
No American school textbook uses the Donner Party to argue about "white culture," "white family breakdown," or "white criminal tendencies."

### REPARABLE CONTRAST
If Black Americans in a similar circumstance had resorted to cannibalism in a white community, the framing would be categorically different.

## SECTION 5: "GATOR BAIT" — BLACK CHILDREN AS CROCODILE FOOD

### THE PRACTICE
Black children in the American South — particularly Florida — were depicted in racist imagery as prey for alligators. This was systematic, commercial, and widespread.

### WHAT IT LOOKED LIKE
- Postcards showing Black children as "gator bait" sold as SOUVENIRS to white tourists in the 1930s
- The phrase "gator bait" became a racial slur for African Americans
- Images depicted Black children as disposable, worthless, suitable only as food for animals
- These were part of a genre called "coon cards" — anti-Black racist ephemera
- Sold at tourist shops throughout Florida and the South

### THE IMAGERY
A postcard from approximately 1930 titled "Alligator Bait, Florida" depicts an alligator eyeing African-American children — implying they were merely sustenance.

### WHO PRODUCED THIS
White American businesses, white photographers, white tourist industry. This was mainstream commercial production targeting white families.

### THE UNIVERSITY OF FLORIDA
In 2020 — the University of Florida BANNED its "Gator Bait" cheer at athletic events after students and alumni pushed back on the racist history.

### THE WIDER CONTEXT
The "gator bait" imagery was part of a broader system of "pickaninny" caricatures depicting Black children as unlovable, disposable, and vulnerable to predators. This imagery was used to normalize the devaluation of Black children's lives.

Sources: Tampa Bay Times, LA Times, Baptist News, Wikipedia (Alligator_bait), The Henry Ford Museum

## SECTION 6: THE DOUBLE STANDARD — WHAT THIS PROVES

### THE FINDING
When white Americans or Europeans practice cannibalism, the framing is:
- "historical curiosity"
- "tragic survival story"
- "desperation"
- "medical practice of the era"
- "dark humor / folklore"

When Black Americans are discussed — even in contexts completely unrelated to cannibalism — the frame is often:
- "pathology"
- "culture of violence"
- "broken families"
- "evidence of inherent dysfunction"
- "proof that Black people are different"

### THE EXAMPLES SIDE BY SIDE

| Event | Who | How It's Framed |

| Jamestown cannibalism (1609) | White English settlers | "Starving Time" — tragic historical episode |
| Alferd Packer (1874) | White American prospector | "The man who ate his friends" — dark folklore |
| Donner Party (1846) | White American settlers | Tragic survival story taught in schools |

| "Gator bait" imagery (1930s) | White businesses targeting white tourists | Souvenirs sold to white families at tourist shops |

### THE SPECIFIC ASYMMETRY
The asymmetry is not that white people are "worse." The asymmetry is in the FRAMING. White acts of extreme violence or dysfunction are treated as isolated historical incidents. Black conditions are treated as evidence of collective pathology.

This is documentably different. The evidence is in the historical record.
